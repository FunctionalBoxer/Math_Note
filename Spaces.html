Map

Let X, Y are sets. Define a function X to Y is a relation \begin{align*} f \subset X \times Y \end{align*} such that

  1. For any x \in X, there exists y \in Y such that (x,y) \in f.

  2. If (x,y) \in f and (x,z) \in f, then y=z.

Denote f as: \begin{align*} f:X \to Y:x \mapsto f(x) \end{align*} Define Image of f by A \subset X: \begin{align*} f[A] \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\{f(a) \mid a \in A\} \subset Y \end{align*} And, Preimage of f by B \subset Y: \begin{align*} f^{-1}[B] \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\{x \in X \mid f(x) \in B\} \subset X \end{align*} f:X \to Y is Injective if: f(x_1) = f(x_2) \implies x_1 = x_2.
f:X \to Y is Surjective if: \forall y \in Y,\ \exists x \in X \text{ s.t. }f(x) = y.
If f is injective and surjective, called bijective.
If f is bijective, then define inverse of f as: \begin{align*} f^{-1}:Y \to X: y \to x \end{align*} where x \in X is the unique elements of X such that f(x) = y.

Let f:X \to Y be a function. Then,

  1. There exists g:Y \to X such that g \circ f:X \to X be an identity function if and only if f is injective.

  2. There exists h:Y \to X such that f \circ h:Y \to Y be an identity function if and only if f is surjective.

Proof.  
1. \implies)
Assume that f(x_1) = f(x_2). Then, existence of left inverse, g(f(x_1)) = g(f(x_2)) \implies x_1 = x_2. Thus f injective.
1. \impliedby)
Since f is injection, for any y \in f[X], there exists a unique element x_y \in X such that f(x) = y. Now, define \begin{align*} g:Y \to X:y \mapsto \begin{cases} x_y \quad &y \in f[X] \\ \text{any element in $X$} \quad & y \notin f[X] \end{cases} \end{align*} Then, for any x \in X, g(f(x)) = g(y) = x.
2. \implies)
Let y \in Y be given. Since existence of right inverse, f(h(y)) = y where h(y) \in X. Thus, f is surjective.
2. \impliedby)
For any y \in Y, there exists a x_y \in X such that f(x_y) = y. Now, define \begin{align*} h:Y \to X: y \mapsto x_y \end{align*} Then, for any y \in Y, f \circ h(y) = f(x_y) = y. Thus, f \circ h is identity. ◻

Isomorphism Theorems

The First Isomorphism Theorem
Let \varphi:G \to H be a Group-Homomorphism. Then, \begin{align*} G / \ker \varphi \cong \varphi[G] \end{align*}

Proof. Let \pi:G \to G/\ker \varphi:x \mapsto x + \ker \varphi. Then, the map \phi:G/\ker \varphi \to \varphi[G]: a + \ker \varphi \mapsto \varphi(a) is isomorphism.
Well-defined and Injective: \begin{align*} a + \ker \varphi = b + \ker \varphi \iff a - b \in \ker \varphi \iff \varphi(a-b) = \varphi(a) - \varphi(b) = 0 \end{align*} Surjective is clear. ◻

The Second Isomorphism Theorem
Let G be a Group, and H \leq G, N \unlhd G. Then, \begin{align*} HN / N \cong H / (H \cap N) \end{align*}

Proof. HK be a subgroup of G, being \begin{align*} HN = \bigcup_{h \in H} hN \overset{N \unlhd G}{=} \bigcup_{h \in H} Nh = NH \end{align*} And, N \leq HN is clear, thus N \unlhd HN.
Meanwhile, H \cap N be a Normal Subgroup of H: for any h \in H, n \in H \cap N, hnh^{-1} \in N because N is normal, and hnh^{-1} \in H since h, n contained in H. Thus, hnh^{-1} \in H \cap N, this implies H \cap N be a Normal of H.
Now, Define a Map: \begin{align*} \varphi:H \to HN / N: h \mapsto hN \end{align*} Clearly, this map is Well-Defined and Homomorphism. And, \begin{align*} \ker \varphi = \varphi^{-1}[1] = \{h \in H \mid hN = N\} = \{h \in H \mid h \in N\} = H \cap N \end{align*} Thus, since The 1st Isomorphism Theorem, \begin{align*} HN / N \cong H / (H \cap N) \end{align*} ◻

The Third Isomorphism Theorem
Let G be a Group, and H, K \unlhd G with H \leq K. Then, K/H \unlhd G/H and \begin{align*} (G/H) / (K/H) \cong (G/ K) \end{align*}

Proof. First, show that K/H \unlhd G/H. Let kH \in K/H and gH \in G/H. Then, \begin{align*} (gH) (kH) (gH)^{-1} = (gH)(kH)(g^{-1}H) = (gkg^{-1})H \in K/H \end{align*} since gkg^{-1} \in K, being K \unlhd G. Now, Define a map: \begin{align*} \varphi:G/H \to G/K:gH \mapsto gK \end{align*} 1. Well-Defined. \begin{align*} g_1H = g_2H \iff g_1^{-1} g_2 \in H \overset{H \leq K}{\implies} g_1^{-1} g_2 \in K \iff g_1K = g_2K \end{align*} 2. Homomorphism.
Clearly, for any g_1H, g_2 \in G/H, \begin{align*} \varphi(g_1H g_2H) = \phi(g_1g_2 H) = g_1g_2 K = g_1K g_2K = \varphi(g_1H) \varphi(g_2H) \end{align*} 3. Surjection. Let gK \in G/K be given. Then, clearly, \varphi(gH) = gK.
4. Kernel. \begin{align*} \ker \varphi = \{gH \in G/H \mid gK = 1\} = \{gH \in G/H \mid g \in K\} = K / H \end{align*} Consequently, The 1st Isomorphism Theorem gives \begin{align*} (G/ K) \cong (G/H) / \ker \varphi = (G/H) / (K/H) \end{align*} ◻

The Forth Isomorphism Theorem
Let G be a Group, and N \unlhd G be a Normal Subgroup. Then, there is a bijection between \begin{align*} D \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\{H \leq G \mid N \leq H\},\ C \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\{\overline{H} \leq G/N\} \end{align*}

Proof. Let \pi:G \to G/N:g \mapsto gN be a natural projection. And, Define \begin{align*} \Phi:D \to C:H \mapsto \pi[H] \end{align*} This function is well-defined: For any H \in D, let aN,bN \in \pi[H]. Then, aN \cdot b^{-1}N = ab^{-1} N \in \pi[H], thus \pi[H] \leq G/N.
To show that one-to-one: Let \Phi(A) = \Phi(B). Thus means, \pi[A] = \pi[B]. Let a \in A. Then, \pi(a) \in \pi[A] = \pi[B], thus \pi(a) = \pi(b) for some b \in B. That is, aN = bN \iff a \in bN. Meanwhile, N \leq B, thus a \in bN \subset B, A \subset B. Similarly, B \subset A, that is A = B.
To show that onto: Let K \in C. Then, N \leq \pi^{-1}[K] \leq G, thus clear. ◻

Cauchy’s Theorem

Cauchy’s Theorem
Let G be a finite group, and p be a prime dividing |G|. Then, G has order p element.

Proof. Define a set: \begin{align*} S \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\{(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_p) \mid x_i \in G,\ x_1 x_2 \cdots x_p = 1\} \end{align*} Then, S has exactly |G|^{p-1} elements because there are |G| possible choices for each of the first p-1 elements in G.
Once x_1, \cdots, x_{p-1} are chosen, then x_p is uniquely determined by the uniqueness of inverses.
Then, let \sigma = (1,2, \dots, p) be a permutation. Then, for any \alpha \in S, \sigma^n(\alpha) \in S for all n \in \mathbb{Z}, being ab = 1 \iff ba = 1.
More precisely, let n \in \mathbb{Z} be given, \alpha = (x_1, \cdots, x_n). Then, \begin{align*} \sigma^n(\alpha) = (x_{n+1},x_{n+2}, \dots, x_p, x_1, x_2, \dots x_{n}) \end{align*} By x_1 \cdots x_n x_{n+1} \cdots x_p = 1, x_{n+1} \cdots x_p x_1 \cdots x_n = 1. Thus \sigma^n(\alpha) \in S. Now, define a relation on S as:

\alpha \sim \beta if and only if \beta = \sigma^n(\alpha) for some n \in \mathbb{Z}

Then, this relation be equivalent relation, thus construct a partition on S. Claim:

[\alpha] = \{\beta \in S \mid \beta \sim \alpha\} is sinlgeton if and only if \alpha = (x, \ldots, x) for some x \in G.

Left direction is clear, and for show that Right direction,
Suppose that \alpha = (x_1, \dots, x_n) has different cordinate elements, let x_i \neq x_j, for some i < j. Then clearly \begin{align*} (x_1, \dots, x_i, \dots, x_p) \neq \sigma^{i-j}(x_1, \dots, x_i, \dots, x_j,\dots, x_p) = (\dots, \underset{\text{i'th element}}{\underline{x_j}}, \dots) \end{align*} Meanwhile, if [\alpha] has elements more than 1, [\alpha] has exactly number of p elements. Because suppose that \alpha = (x_1, \dots, x_p) has at least one different coordinate. Then, \begin{align*} \sigma^1(\alpha), \sigma^2(\alpha), \cdots, \sigma^{p-1}(\alpha) \end{align*} are mutually different: If there exist 1 \leq i < j < p such that \sigma^i(\alpha) = \sigma^j(\alpha), that is, \sigma^{j-i}(\alpha) = \alpha.
Now, j - i \mid p, this is contradiction with p is prime. Therefore, every equivalent class has order 1 or p. Consequently, \begin{align*} |G|^{p-1} = k + pd \end{align*} where k is a number of classes of size 1, and d is a number of classes of size p. And (1,1,\dots, 1) \in S, k is at least 1.
Since p divides |G|^{p-1} = k + pd, thus k must be bigger than 1, thus there exists elements such that x^p = 1. ◻

Sylow’s Theorem

Sylow’s Theorem
Let G be a group of order p^\alpha m, where p is a prime such that p \nmid m.
A group of order p^r,\ (r \geq 1) is called a p-group, Subgroups of G which are p-groups are called p-subgroup.
In particular, subgroups of order p^\alpha is called Sylow p-subgroup of G. And, define a collection \begin{align*} \text{Syl}_p(G) \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\{P \leq G \mid |P| = p^\alpha\},\ n_p(G) \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\text{Card}(\text{Syl}_p(G)) \end{align*}

  1. The First Sylow Theorem
    There exists a Sylow p-subgroup of G. i.e., \text{Syl}_p(G) \neq \emptyset.

  2. The Second Sylow Theorem
    If P \in \text{Syl}_p(G) and Q \leq G be a p-subgroup. Then, there exists g \in G such that Q \leq g P g^{-1}.

  3. The Third Sylow Theorem
    n_p \equiv 1 (\bmod p), n_p = |G : N_G(P)| for any P \in \text{Syl}_p(G), and n_p \mid m.

Before prove above statments, we show that:

Let P \in \text{Syl}_p(G). If Q is p-subgroup of G, then Q \cap N_G(P) = Q \cap P.

Proof. Put H = Q \cap N_G(P). Since P \leq G, for any p \in P, pPp^{-1} = P, thus p \in N_G(P). i.e., P \leq N_G(P).
Thus, Enough to Show that H \leq Q \cap P. Since H \leq N_G(P), \begin{align*} PH = \bigcup_{h \in H} Ph = \bigcup_{h \in H} hP = HP \end{align*} Thus, PH \leq G. And, \begin{align*} |PH| = \frac{|P||H|}{|P \cap H|} \end{align*} By Lagrange’s Theorem, H \leq P and P \cap H \leq P must have order of powers of p, so PH be a p-group. Clearly, P \leq PH and P is the largest p-group of G, thus, PH = P. This means, H \leq P. ◻

Proof. The First Theorem: The existence of Sylow p-subgroup. Proof by Induction:
If |G| = 1, there is nothing to prove.
Assume inductively the existence of Sylow p-subgroups for all groups of order less than |G|.
In case of p | |Z(G)|, then by Cauchy’s Theorem, Z(G) has a subgroup N which has order of p.
Clearly N is Normal, and G / N = |G| / |N| = p^{a-1}m. By assumption, G/N has a subgroup P' of order p^{\alpha - 1}.
By The Forth Isomorphism Theorem, Let P \leq G be a subgroup such that P / N = P'.
Then, |P| = |P / N| \cdot |N| = p^{\alpha}, Thus P be a Sylow p-subgroup of G.
In case of p \nmid |Z(G)|.
Let g_1, \dots, g_r be represectatives of the distinct conjugacy classes of G, not contained in Z(G). Then, The Class Equation gives \begin{align*} |G| = |Z(G)| + \sum_{i=1}^{r} |G:C_G(g_i)| \end{align*} Since p divides |G|, if for all i = 1, 2, \dots, r, p \mid |G:C_G(g_i)| then p \mid |Z(G)|, this is contradiction.
Thus, for some j, p \nmid |G:C_G(g_j)|. Put H = C_G(g_j) < G. Then, |H| has a factor of p^\alpha, by p \nmid |G:C_G(g_j)|. Now, \begin{align*} |H| = p^\alpha m' \quad(m' < m) \end{align*} By assumption, H has a Sylow p-group, order of p^\alpha.
Consequently, the existence of Sylow p-subgroup was shown.
The Second Theorem: Relation of p-subgroups.
The First Theorem gives existence of Sylow p-subgroups. Let P \in \text{Syl}_p(G). Denote that: \begin{align*} S \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\{gPg^{-1} \mid g \in G\} = \{P_1, \ldots, P_r\} \end{align*} Let Q \leq G be an any p-subgroup of G. And, Q acts by conjucation on S. i.e., \begin{align*} \alpha:Q \times S \to S: (q, P_i) \mapsto qP_i q^{-1} \end{align*} Write S as a disjoint union of orbits under this action by Q: \begin{align*} S = \mathcal{O}_1 \cup \mathcal{O}_2 \cup \cdots \cup \mathcal{O}_s \end{align*} where r = |\mathcal{O}_1| + \cdots + |\mathcal{O}_s|. Rearrange a set S as: P_i \in \mathcal{O}_i,\ 1 \leq i \leq s. Now, using Definition, Lemma, and above Theorem, \begin{align*} |\mathcal{O}_i| \overset{\text{Thm}}{=} |Q : N_Q(P_i)| \overset{\text{def}}{=} |Q : N_G(P_i) \cap Q| \overset{\text{lemma}}{=} |Q : P_i \cap Q| \end{align*} for each 1 \leq i \leq s. Since Q was arbitrary, Let Q = P_1, so that |\mathcal{O}_1| = |P_1 : P_1 \cap P_1| = 1. And, for each i \geq 2, P_i \cap P_1 < P_1, \begin{align*} |\mathcal{O}_i| = |P_1 : P_i \cap P_1| > 1 \end{align*} Since P_1 \in \text{Syl}_p(G), that is |P_1| = p^\alpha, |P_1 : P_i \cap P_1| = |P_1|/|P_i \cap P_1| = p^k where 1 \leq k < \alpha.
This means for each 2 \leq i \leq s, p divides |\mathcal{O}_i|. Thus, \begin{align*} r = |\mathcal{O}_1| + (|\mathcal{O}_2| + \cdots + |\mathcal{O}_s|) \equiv 1 (\bmod p) \end{align*} Now, Proof by Contradiction: Let Q\leq G be a p-subgroup. Suppose that for any 1 \leq i \leq r, Q \nleq P_i.
Then, P_i \cap Q < Q for all i, this means \begin{align*} |\mathcal{O}_i| = |Q : P_i \cap Q| > 1 \end{align*} Thus for any i, p divides |\mathcal{O}_i|, this is Contradiction. This proved Relation of p-subgroups.
Finally, The Third Theorem:
Since Second Theorem, this gives that S = \text{Syl}_p(G), thus n_p(G) = r. That is, n_p \equiv 1 (\bmod p).
Since all Sylow p-subgroups are Conjugate, for any P \in \text{Syl}_p(G), \begin{align*} n_p = r = |\mathcal{O}_1| = |G:N_G(P)| \end{align*} Consequently, Completing the Sylow Theorem. ◻

Ring of Fractions

Let R be a Commutative Ring, D \subset R be a subset such that \begin{cases} \text{no zero, no zero divisors} \\ \text{closed under multiplication} \end{cases}.
Then, there exists a Commutative Ring Q with identity satisfies:

  1. R can embed in Q, and every element of D becomes unit in Q. More precisely, Q = \{r d^{-1} \mid r \in R,\ d \in D\}.

  2. Q is the smallest Ring contaning R with identity such that every element of D becomes unit in Q.

Proof. Let \mathcal{F} \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\{(r,d) \mid r \in R,\ d \in D\} and the relation \sim on \mathcal{F} by (r_1, d_1) \sim (r_2, d_2) \iff r_1d_2 = r_2d_1.
Then, \sim is equivalent relation: reflexive and symmetirc are clear, and Suppose that (r_1, d_1) \sim (r_2, d_2) and (r_2, d_2) \sim (r_3, d_3). \begin{align*} r_2d_3 = r_3d_2 \implies r_2 d_1 d_3 = r_3 d_1 d_2 \implies r_1 d_2 d_3 = r_3 d_1 d_2 \implies d_2 ( r_1 d_3 - r_3 d_1) \implies r_1d_3 = r_3d_1 \end{align*} Thus transitivity shown. Define \begin{align*} \frac{r}{d} \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}[(r,d)] = \{(a,b) \mid (a,b) \sim (r,d) \},\ Q \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\left\{ \frac{r}{d} \;\middle|\; r \in R,\ d \in D \right\} \end{align*} And define operations +, \times on Q: \begin{align*} \frac{r_1}{d_1} + \frac{r_2}{d_2} \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\frac{r_1d_2 + r_2d_1}{d_1 d_2}, \quad \frac{r_1}{d_1} \times \frac{r_2}{d_2} \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\frac{r_1r_2}{d_1d_2} \end{align*} Well-Definedness: If \displaystyle\frac{r_1}{d_1} = \frac{r'_1}{d'_1} and \displaystyle\frac{r_2}{d_2} = \frac{r'_2}{d'_2}, \begin{align*} \frac{r_1d_2 + r_2d_1}{d_1 d_2} = \frac{r_1 d_2 d'_1 d'_2 + r_2 d_1 d'_1 d'_2}{d_1 d_2 d'_1 d'_2} = \frac{(r_1 d'_1) d_2 d'_2 + (r_2 d'_2) d_1 d'_1 }{d_1 d_2 d'_1 d'_2} = \frac{(r'_1 d_1) d_2 d'_2 + (r'_2 d_2) d_1 d'_1 }{d_1 d_2 d'_1 d'_2} = \frac{(r'_1d'_2 + r'_2d'_1)d_1d_2}{d_1d_2 d'_1 d'_2} = \frac{r'_1d'_2 + r'_2d'_1}{d'_1 d'_2} \end{align*} \begin{align*} \frac{r_1r_2}{d_1d_2} = \frac{r_1 r_2 d'_1 d'_2}{d_1d_2d'_1d'_2} = \frac{(r_1 d'_1) (r_2 d'_2)}{d_1d_2d'_1d'_2} = \frac{(r'_1 d_1) (r'_2 d_2)}{d_1d_2d'_1d'_2} = \frac{r'_1 r'_2 d_1 d_2}{d_1d_2d'_1d'_2} = \frac{r'_1 r'_2}{d'_1d'_2} \end{align*} Now, (Q, +, \times) constructs Commutative Ring with identity: for any d \in D, put \displaystyle 0_Q \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\frac{0}{d},\ 1_Q \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\frac{d}{d}. Then,

  1. (R, +, \times) closed under the operations since D is closed under the multiplication.

  2. (R, +) has a zero: \displaystyle\frac{r_1}{d_1} + 0_Q = \frac{r_1}{d_1} + \frac{0}{d} = \frac{r_1 d + 0 d_1}{d_1 d} = \frac{r_1 d}{d_1 d} = \frac{r_1}{d_1}.

A Topological Space X is compact if: every open cover contains a finite subcover. i.e.,

If \displaystyle X = \bigcup_{\alpha \in \Lambda} \mathcal{U}_\alpha, (\mathcal{U}_\alpha \in \mathcal{T}), then there is finite subcover such that \displaystyle X = \bigcup_{i=1}^N \mathcal{U}_{\alpha_i}

This is equivalent with:

If \displaystyle\emptyset = \bigcap_{\alpha \in \Lambda} \mathcal{C}_\alpha, (\mathcal{C}_\alpha \text{ closed}), then there is finite subset such that \displaystyle\emptyset = \bigcap_{i=1}^N \mathcal{C}_{\alpha_i}

Let X be a set. A \subset \mathcal{P}(X) satisfies finite intersection property if:

For all finite subset of A, \{A_i \mid i = 1, 2, \dots, n\} \subset A satisfies \displaystyle\bigcap_{i=1}^n A_i \neq \emptyset.

Step 2. Construct an Urysohn Function.

Then, this map f is well-defined by (*) and \sup D \leq 1. And f satisfies that:

  1. \forall r \in D,\ x \in A \subset U_r. Thus, f(x) = 0 if x \in A.

Tietze Extension Theorem

Tietze Extension Theroem
Let X be a T_4 Space, and A \subseteq X be a closed subset.
For any Continuous map f:A \to \mathbb{R}, there exists a Continuous map: \begin{align*} g:X \to \mathbb{R} \ \text{ s.t. }\ g|_A = f \end{align*} This g is called extension of f.

Proof. This proof consists by three steps.
Step 1. First, we will show that: \begin{align*} \text{For any Continuous map $f:A \to [-r, r]$, there is a Continuous map $h:X \to \mathbb{R}$} \text{ s.t. } \begin{cases} \forall x \in X,\ |h(x)| \leq \frac{1}{3}r \\ \forall a \in A,\ |f(a) - h(a)| \leq \frac{2}{3}r \end{cases} \tag{$*$} \label{contimaprr} \end{align*} Set \begin{align*} I_1 \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\left[-r, -\frac{1}{3}r\right],\ I_2 \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\left[-\frac{1}{3}r, \frac{1}{3}r\right],\ I_3 \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\left[\frac{1}{3}r, r\right] \end{align*} Then, the preimage of continuous map preserves closed and A is closed subspace of X, f^{-1}[I_1] and f^{-1}[I_3] are closed of X.
And, I_1 and I_3 are disjoint, thus f^{-1}[I_1 \cap I_3] = f^{-1}[I_1] \cap f^{-1}[I_3] = \emptyset.
Now, apply the Urysohn Lemma: There exists an Urysohn function h:X \to I_2 for f^{-1}[I_1] and f^{-1}[I_3].
Clearly, this map h satisfies the first condition in (*). And, for show the second condition, let a \in A be given.
If a \in f^{-1}[I_1], then f(a) \in I_1 and h(a) = -\frac{1}{3}r, thus |f(a) - h(a)| \leq \frac{2}{3}r.
If a \in f^{-1}[I_3], then f(a) \in I_3 and h(a) = \frac{1}{3}r, thus |f(a) - h(a)| \leq \frac{2}{3}r.
If a \notin (f^{-1}[I_1]\cup f^{-1}[I_3]), then f(a),h(a) \in I_2, thus |f(a) - h(a)| \leq \frac{2}{3}r.
Therefore, the second condition satisfied.
Step 2. We will show that: for any f:A \to [-1,1], there exists an extension of f.
Apply the result in Step 1, there exists a Continuous map: \begin{align*} h_1:X \to \mathbb{R} \text{ s.t. }\begin{cases} \forall x \in X,\ |h_1(x)| \leq \frac{1}{3} \\ \forall a \in A,\ |f(a) - h_1(a)| \leq \frac{2}{3} \end{cases} \end{align*} Now, the second condition of h_1, the continuous map f-h_1:A \to \left[-\frac{2}{3}, \frac{2}{3}\right]:x \to f(x) - h_1(x) is well-defined.
Again, there exists a Continuous map: \begin{align*} h_2:X \to \mathbb{R} \text{ s.t. } \begin{cases} \forall x \in X,\ |h_2(x)| \leq \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{2}{3}\\ \forall a \in A,\ |f(a) - h_1(a) - h_2(a)| \leq \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^2 \end{cases} \end{align*} Inductively, for any n \in \mathbb{N}, there exists a Continuous map: \begin{align*} h_n:X \to \mathbb{R} \text{ s.t. } \begin{cases} \forall x \in X,\ |h_n(x)| \leq \frac{1}{3} \cdot \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{n-1}\\ \forall a \in A,\ |f(a) - h_1(a) - h_2(a) - \cdots - h_n(a)| \leq \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n \end{cases} \end{align*} Define a map \begin{align*} g:X \to [-1,1]:x \mapsto \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} h_n(x) \end{align*} For any x \in X, \begin{align*} |g(x)| = \left| \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} h_n(x) \right| \leq \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |h_n(x)| \leq \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{3} \cdot \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{n-1} = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - \frac{2}{3}} = 1 \end{align*} Therefore, this map is well-defined. And, Weierstrass M-test gives that \displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} h_n(x) converges uniformly.
Moreover, for any a \in A, \begin{align*} \left|f(a) - \sum_{k=1}^{n} h_k(a) \right| \leq \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n \implies \left|f(a) - \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} h_n(a) \right| = \left|f(a) - g(a) \right| = 0 \end{align*} That is, g is Continuous on X and g|_A = f. Therefore, g is extension of f.
Step 3. Finally, we generalize the result in Step 2.:
Let f:A \to [a,b] be a Continuous map on the closed subspace A. And, let \varphi:[a,b] \to [-1,1] be a Homeomorphism.
Then, \varphi \circ f:A \to [-1,1] is Continuous map, thus there exists an extension g:X \to [-1, 1] such that g|_A = \varphi \circ f.
Now, \varphi^{-1} \circ g:X \to [a, b] is Continuous, and (\varphi^{-1} \circ g)|_A = \varphi^{-1} \circ \varphi \circ f = f, Therefore this \varphi^{-1} \circ g is the extension of f.
Let f:A \to \mathbb{R} be a Continuous map on the closed subspace A.
And, let \varphi:\mathbb{R} \to (-1,1) be a Homeomorphism. Then, the map \phi:\mathbb{R} \to [-1, 1]: x \mapsto \varphi(x) is still Continuous.
Now, The Continuous map \phi \circ f:A \to [-1,1] has an extension g:X \to [-1,1] such that g|_A = \phi \circ f.
Put B = g^{-1}[\{-1, 1\}]. Then B is Closed on X, and A \cap B = \emptyset. Now, apply the Urysohn Lemma to this,
there exists an Urysohn function for A and B: Continuous map \gamma:X \to [0,1] such that \gamma|_A = 1 and \gamma|_B = 0.
Define a map \eta:X \to (-1, 1):x \mapsto g(x) \gamma(x). Then, if g(x) = 1 or g(x) = -1, then x \in B, thus g(x) \gamma(x) = 0.
Therfore, \eta is well-defined. And, for any a \in A, \eta(a) = g(a) \gamma(a) = g(a), thus \eta|_A = \phi \circ f.
Consequently, the map \phi^{-1}\circ \eta is an extension of f, we wanted. ◻

Taylor’s Theorem

Definition

Let f:(a,b) \to \mathbb{R} be a Real-valued function. f is said to be convex if: For any x,y \in (a,b), \lambda \in (0,1), \begin{align*} f(\lambda x + (1 - \lambda)y) \leq \lambda f(x) + (1 - \lambda) f(y) \end{align*} Convex function has follwing properties:

Let f:(a,b) \to \mathbb{R} be a Convex function, and a < x_1 < x_2 < x_3 < b. Then, \begin{align*} \frac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1} \leq \frac{f(x_3) - f(x_1)}{x_3 - x_1} \leq \frac{f(x_3) - f(x_2)}{x_3 - x_2} \end{align*}

Proof. To show that first inequalty, note that \begin{align*} \frac{x_2 - x_1}{x_3 - x_1} \cdot x_3 + \left( 1 - \frac{x_2 - x_1}{x_3 - x_1} \right) \cdot x_1 = \frac{(x_2 - x_1) x_3 + (x_3 - x_2) x_1}{x_3 - x_1} = x_2 \end{align*} Now, \begin{align*} f(x_2) &= f\left(\frac{x_2 - x_1}{x_3 - x_1} \cdot x_3 + \left( 1 - \frac{x_2 - x_1}{x_3 - x_1} \right) \cdot x_1\right) \\ &\leq \frac{x_2 - x_1}{x_3 - x_1} \cdot f\left(x_3 \right) + \left( 1 - \frac{x_2 - x_1}{x_3 - x_1} \right) \cdot f\left( x_1 \right) = \frac{x_2 - x_1}{x_3 - x_1} \cdot f\left(x_3 \right) + f(x_1) - \left(\frac{x_2 - x_1}{x_3 - x_1} \right) \cdot f\left( x_1 \right) \end{align*} In brief, \begin{align*} f(x_2) - f(x_1) \leq \frac{x_2 - x_1}{x_3 - x_1} (f(x_3) - f(x_1)) \implies \frac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1} \leq \frac{f(x_3) - f(x_1)}{x_3 - x_1} \end{align*} And similarly, \begin{align*} \frac{x_3 - x_2}{x_3 - x_1} \cdot x_1 + \left(1 - \frac{x_3 - x_2}{x_3 - x_1}\right) x_3 = x_2 \end{align*} gives the second inequality. ◻

Lipschitz Condition

Definition

A real-vauled function f:(a,b) \to \mathbb{R} is called Lipschitz Continuous if: \begin{align*} \exists L \geq 0 \text{ s.t. }\forall x_1, x_2 \in (a,b),\ |f(x_1) - f(x_2)| \leq L \cdot |x_1 - x_2| \end{align*} The constant L is said to be Lipschitz Constant of f. In particular, the constant \begin{align*} D \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}\sup_{x_1 \neq x_2} \frac{|f(x_1) - f(x_2)|}{|x_1 - x_2|} \end{align*} is called dilation of f. Clearly, \begin{align*} \forall x_1, x_2 \in (a,b),\ |f(x_1) - f(x_2)| \leq D \cdot |x_1 - x_2| \end{align*} and if L > 0 is Lipschitz Constant of f, then D \leq L. That is, D = \inf \{L > 0 \mid \text{$L$ is Lipschitz constant of $f$}\}.

Newton-Raphson Method
Let f:[a,b] \to \mathbb{R} be a twice-differentiable, f(a) < 0 < f(b). Suppose that f satisfies: for all x \in [a,b], \begin{align*} f'(x) \geq \delta > 0 \text{ and } 0 \leq f''(x) \leq M \end{align*} That is, f is strictly increasing convex function, and Lipschitz Continuous.
Further, there uniquely exists x^* \in (a,b) such that f(x^*) = 0.
Let x_1 \in (x^*, b) fixed. Define a sequence \{x_n\} inductively as follows: \begin{align*} x_{n+1} \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)} \end{align*} Then, \{x_n\} satisfies the following three conditions:

  1. \{x_n\} is decreasing sequence.

  2. x_n \to x^* as n \to \infty.

  3. For any n \in \mathbb{N}, 0 \leq x_{n+1} - x^* \leq \left[ \frac{M}{2\delta} \right]^{2^{n+1} - 1} [x_1 - x^*]^{2^n}.

Condition 3 means that for a suitable initial value x_1, we can establish an upper bound for the error.

Proof. This proof consists by three steps.
Since f'' is non-negative, and f' is positive, f is strictly increasing convex function.
And Fundamental Theorem of Calculus gives: for any x \in (a,b), \begin{align*} f'(x) \overset{\text{FTA}}{=} \int_{a}^{x} f''(t) dt + f'(a) \leq \int_{a}^{x} M dt + f'(a) = M(x - a) + f'(a) \leq M (b - a) + f'(a) \end{align*} Thus, f' is bounded on (a,b), thus f is Lipschitz Continuous.
Step 1. f has a unique root x^*.
The existence of root given directly by Intermidate-Value theroem.
Suppose that x^*, x' \in (a,b) are distinct root of f. i.e., f(x^*) = f(x') = 0. Then, by Mean-value theroem, there is c \in (a,b) between x^* and x' such that \begin{align*} f'(c) (x^* - x') = f(x^*) - f(x') = 0 \end{align*} That is, f'(c) = 0. This is contradiction with f' is positive.
Step 2. \{x_n\} decrease.
Proof by induction:
For n = 1, f'(x_1)(x_1 - x_2) \overset{\textnormal{def}}{=}f(x_1) > f(x^*) = 0, thus x_2 < x_1. Proof. Hölder’s Inequality

Minkowski inequality
Let f, g \in \mathcal{F}. Then, for any p \geq 1, \|f + g\|_p \leq \|f\|_p + \|g\|_p.
